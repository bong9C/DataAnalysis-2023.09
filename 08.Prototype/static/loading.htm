Loading Gif – Research (1)

I began my research into loading gifs by simply googling loading gifs. I chose the ones appealed to me the most.

Simple:

 

 

The two that caught my eye the most where the bottom two. I like the use of the shadow beneath the boxes, it makes a 3D effect which makes it look more complicated. These type of loading gifs can be found across most programmes to signify a period of loading.

Look simple but technically more complicated:

I then looked in to smoother and more creative gifs. The dripping zig zag and the orange infinity circle popped out the most. I break these down below to see how the motion of the gif is working.

 

 

 

 

I wanted to see how this effect was created so opened it up in photoshop. Altogether 215 frames were used to create this gif.

I then looked at some frames across a section of the gif to see how it works. It starts as a simple z shape with a droplet that gets smaller. Another droplet forms off the bottom of the z shape it ‘grows’ from the top. So overall it grows in a similar zig zag shape while the zig zag ‘disintegrates’ at the bottom. This obviously took a lot of time to create the fluid overall look.

This gif was created using 104 frames – half of what the above took.

I broke this down and found that it started with the figure of 8 loop. The right side gets squashed down and stretched out, which then forms 3 loops.

Then to get back to the figure of 8 (but reflected), the 3 loops then get squashed from the left. This slowly becomes 2 loops again. This is then repeated to get back to the figure of 8 it started with (not reflected).

Animated illustrations:

I then started to look at more illustrative loading gifs. They still signify a passing of time, but have more elements going on than the previous ones. These are breaking away from the loading gifs into animated logos. I break down the top right one below to see how they achieve this movement.

 

I broke down the gif below in photoshop. This surprisingly only used 56 frames. Much less than the ones I broke down above even though I thought they were simpler.

The first step was the sun coming up. Once at the point of the second screen shot the whole semi circles rotates clockwise slightly then back to central.

Then it rotates anticlockwise whilst the opacity of the green semi circle gets lower. A band of blue then reveals the mountains more. The opacity is then higher whilst the dark blue semi circle is built up, whilst the moon travels from the right to the left of the picture.

I then took a closer look at the mountains. To get the ‘boing’ effect as it spins round and stops, the mountains are changed in height. This gives the appearance of them jumping because of the motion and then settling back down once the image is complete.

This gif has complicated elements like changing the opacity to revel the blue underside etc. But it is not complicated as it is not looped. Once the blue scene is revealed it simple cuts back to the green start. The blue doesn’t ‘transform’ back to the green.

 

Overall it was really helpful to look in to the processes of how these gifs worked. Breaking it down into simple steps made me think about how it was created and not just looking at it and thinking it would be too complicated for me to create something similar. It also made me realise that even though something that looks technically more complicated doesn’t necessarily mean it took more frames to make.
